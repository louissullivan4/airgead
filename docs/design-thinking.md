
IBM Design Thinking - The App Idea, v2 (Accountant-First)
The frame
Users · Restless Reinvention · Diverse Empowered Teams. The market check was a restless-reinvention moment - the first idea met reality and changed. Good. As a solo founder, "diverse teams" = go get accountants to disagree with you before you build.
The strategic pivot anchoring everything below: you are not building a receipt scanner for sole traders. You are building the tool an Irish accountant uses to run their book of small/equine clients - where the client's capture app is the bait, and the accountant's saved time is the business. The camera/OCR is plumbing, not the point. The bank feed and the incumbents (QuickBooks, Surf/BrightBooks, Sort My Books) own the trader-first, horizontal space; you win by owning the accountant's workflow in a vertical niche they neglect.

Step 1 - Hills (rewritten)
Hill 1 - the accountant (the hero and buyer): An accountant can run an entire book of sole-trader clients from one place and reach tax season with every client's records already clean, complete, and export-ready - with nothing to chase, because the data arrived structured all year.
Hill 2 - the sole trader (the seat, not the hero): A self-employed person can keep their accountant happy with near-zero effort - with capture so frictionless it's a 5-second habit, not an accounting task they have to understand.
Hill 3 - the niche wedge: An Irish equine/agricultural business and its accountant can handle the genuinely fiddly stuff - capital allowances on equipment, livestock, motor/mileage, flat-rate farmer VAT - with a tool built for their tax reality, not a generic one they have to bend.
Hill 4 - the regulatory tailwind (medium-term): A small practice can get its whole client base ready for Ireland's mandatory e-invoicing and real-time reporting (2028–2029) with one tool, instead of scrambling when the mandate lands.
The ordering matters now. Hill 1 is the business. Hill 2 is what makes Hill 1 possible (the accountant only wins if the trader actually captures). Hill 3 is where you start - the beachhead. Hill 4 is the rising tide you position into. Notice the trader Hill is now explicitly subordinate - "keep their accountant happy" - because the trader's motivation in an accountant-first model is social/relational, not self-serving.

Step 2 - Empathy maps (re-centred)
Persona A (now primary) - Liam, accountant. Small Irish practice, 40–80 clients, several equine/farm.

Says: "Just send me everything, I'll sort it." "I lose half of January chasing receipts." "I bill for data entry but I'd rather not do it." "The farm clients are the messiest - and the fiddliest."
Thinks: Every client who hands me clean data is an hour back at the worst time of year. I'd push a tool that does that - if it doesn't make me look bad to my clients and doesn't add work for me.
Does: Receives shoeboxes, WhatsApp photos, PDFs; re-keys into Sage/Surf; chases gaps; eats the peak-season crunch personally.
Feels: Bottlenecked, undifferentiated from every other practice, resentful of low-value work, anxious about the coming e-invoicing mandate.
The real pain: His scarcest, most expensive resource is his own hours at peak season, consumed by clients' mess and by fiddly niche cases. He has budget and motive. He is also a distribution channel - one Liam brings 40 traders.

Persona B (now secondary) - Niamh, sole trader (equine business).

Says: "My accountant sorts the tax stuff." "I'll deal with receipts later." "I don't really understand the tax side and don't want to."
Thinks: I just don't want a row with my accountant or a scary letter from Revenue. Make it not my problem.
Does: Stuffs receipts away, photographs a few, dumps it all on Liam at year-end.
Feels: Avoidant about admin, vaguely guilty, trusting of (and slightly deferential to) her accountant.
The real pain: Not "I want better expense software" - she doesn't. It's "I don't want this to be my job or my worry." She'll adopt a tool because Liam asked her to and it's trivially easy, not because she went looking.

The critical reframe between these two maps: Niamh will never be your customer in the acquisition sense. She won't search for you, compare you to QuickBooks, or care about features. She adopts because her trusted accountant hands her the app and it takes five seconds. That's why accountant-first works and trader-first doesn't - you were trying to acquire the person who doesn't buy, to beat incumbents at the thing they already do.

Step 5 - Big Ideas, re-prioritised (impact × effort)
High impact / where the moat is - build these well:

Accountant dashboard: whole book in one view, per-client status, one-click export (Hill 1 - the actual product)
One-click client onboarding / invite (the distribution mechanism; if onboarding is hard, the channel clogs)
Niche tax fit: equine/agricultural categories, capital allowances, flat-rate VAT (Hill 3 - the defensible wedge)

High impact / higher effort - the business and the tailwind:

Accountant-led pricing: free for the accountant, per-seat for clients (the model with a moat)
E-invoicing / real-time-reporting readiness (Hill 4 - position now, deliver as mandate nears)
Sage / Surf / ROS-friendly export (meeting the Irish stack where it is)

Table-stakes plumbing - necessary, not differentiating, don't fall in love:

Camera-first capture + OCR auto-fill (Hill 2's mechanism - has to be frictionless, doesn't have to be novel)
Storage/cost optimisation (only matters at volume; the cleanup step banks most of it for free)

The thing to actively resist: competing horizontally on capture features against QuickBooks et al. The 2×2 now says plainly - your impact is concentrated in the accountant overview and the niche fit, the two things incumbents do worst. Every hour spent making the camera marginally slicker instead of making Liam's dashboard indispensable is an hour spent on the commodity.
One honest correction from v1: the bank feed. Much of a trader's spend already flows through their bank/card, and incumbents auto-import it. You don't need to build bank feeds to start (niche + accountant workflow is the wedge), but be clear-eyed that photos are the evidence layer, not the capture layer. Long-term, "attach the receipt photo to the bank transaction Revenue might audit" is the truer framing than "snap every receipt."

Step 6 - Storyboard of the core Hill (Hill 1 - Liam's wow)

Liam at his desk, new client signs on. He pastes an email, hits invite. "Onboarding a client is one click."
Niamh's phone buzzes - "Your accountant Liam invited you." She taps, it's set up. "The trader joins because the accountant asked - not because she shopped for it."
Months pass, a quiet montage - Niamh snapping the odd receipt in 5 seconds, transactions quietly accumulating, clean. "Capture is trivial, so it actually happens."
An equine-specific receipt - feed, vet, equipment - and the tool already knows it's a capital-allowance item, not a straight expense. "The fiddly niche stuff is pre-sorted."
Tax season. Liam opens his dashboard. 40 clients, nearly all green/complete. "The wow: nothing to chase."
One click: export to Sage (and a badge: "e-invoice ready for 2028"). "The afternoon that used to be January."

What the storyboard exposes about this idea:

Panel 1 is make-or-break and easy to under-invest in. If accountant onboarding of clients has any friction, the whole distribution model stalls - this is more important than any capture feature.
Panel 2 reveals the trust transfer: the trader adopts on the accountant's authority. Your trader-side UX job is "don't break that trust and don't make it hard," not "delight a power user."
Panel 4 is the moat and the reason to go niche-first - a generic tool skips it; you win the equine accountant precisely because you didn't.
Panel 5 is the entire product compressed into one image. If you can't make Liam's dashboard deliver that feeling, nothing else matters.


Step 7 - Playback (the part only you can do - and now it's pointed at the right person)
v1's Playback pointed at sole traders. That was wrong, and the market check is why. Point it at accountants.
Take Hill 1 and this storyboard to 3–5 small Irish accountants, ideally some with farm/equine clients, and test the assumptions that actually decide the business:

The buyer assumption: "If I gave you this free, and your clients paid €X/seat, would you push your whole book onto it?" If they shrug and say "they just use QuickBooks / Surf," the model's dead - learn that for the price of a coffee, not a year of building.
The distribution assumption: "How many clients would you realistically onboard, and how fast?" One Liam = 40 seats or 2 seats. That number is your entire unit economics.
The moat assumption: "Is the equine/farm tax fiddliness real enough that a tool built for it would make you choose it over the generic suites?" If the niche pain isn't sharp, you have no wedge and you're back to fighting incumbents horizontally.
The trader-adoption assumption (secondary): "If you asked a client to use a 5-second capture app, would they actually do it?" Tests whether Niamh adopts on Liam's authority.

The one assumption that, if false, collapses this idea: "Would accountants actively distribute this to their clients?" The entire model rests on the accountant being a willing channel. If they won't push it - because it threatens their billable data-entry hours, or because they don't want to be tech support for clients, or because they're loyal to Sage - then accountant-first dies too, and you'd need a third model. That's the question. Ask it first, ask it bluntly.

Two closing flags, kept honest. First: this v2 is stronger than v1 because v1 got falsified by real market data - which is the whole point of the method, and a reminder that this v2 deserves the same skepticism until real accountants confirm it. Second: the through-line is now unmistakable and worth tattooing on the wall - the accountant is the customer, the niche is the wedge, the dashboard "nothing to chase" moment is the wow, and the camera is just plumbing. If you ever catch yourself most excited about the camera again, you've drifted back toward the version the market already told us is weak.

I've written it to actively hunt for the "no," because a cheap no now is worth more than an expensive yes later.
Three rules before the questions, because they matter more than the questions:
Don't pitch. Interview. The moment you describe your app, they'll be polite and tell you it sounds great. Politeness is poison to validation. Spend the first two-thirds asking about their current reality before you ever mention what you're building. You're a researcher, not a salesperson.
Ask about the past, not the future. "Would you use X?" invites a fantasy answer. "Walk me through what you did last January" gets you the truth. Past behaviour predicts; hypothetical enthusiasm doesn't.
Shut up. After you ask, stop talking. Silence makes them fill it with the real answer. Your job is to follow their thread with "why?" and "tell me more about that," not to move to the next question.

The script
Part 0 - Framing (30 seconds, say this verbatim-ish)
"I'm trying to understand how small accounting practices handle sole-trader clients' records - I'm not selling anything today, I genuinely just want to learn how it works for you. There are no right answers, and it's most useful to me when you're blunt. Mind if I ask about how things actually run?"
This lowers their guard and licenses honesty. If you skip it, everything after is contaminated by "this person wants me to like their thing."
Part 1 - Their world (the chase pain - Hill 1)
Pure past-tense behaviour. No mention of your idea.

"How many sole-trader clients do you have, roughly?"
"Walk me through what last tax season actually looked like for you - start to finish."
"When a client's records come in, what shape are they in?" (Listen for shoebox/WhatsApp/PDF chaos - or for "actually they're fine because they all use Xero," which is a yellow flag for your premise.)
"How much of your January is chasing missing things versus actual accounting?"
"What part of that whole process do you most wish you didn't have to do?"

What you're testing: is the chase/re-keying pain as sharp as you think, or have bank feeds and existing software already solved most of it? If they say "honestly it's mostly fine now," that's the most important thing you'll hear all day - don't argue with it.
Part 2 - The niche (Hill 3, the moat)
Only if they have farm/equine/agricultural clients.

"Are some types of client more painful than others? Which, and why?"
"Tell me about the last farm or equine client you did - what made it fiddly?" (Listen for capital allowances, livestock, flat-rate VAT, mileage - the things you'd build for.)
"Do the tools you use today handle that well, or do you work around them by hand?"

What you're testing: is the niche pain real and tool-shaped, or is it just "tax is complicated everywhere"? If they don't light up here, your wedge is dull.
Part 3 - Current tools (the competition reality)

"What software are you and your clients on today?" (QuickBooks, Surf/BrightBooks, Sort My Books, Sage, ROS…)
"How did your clients end up on those - did you put them there, or did they arrive with them?"
"If you wanted a client to start using a particular tool, would they? Have you ever done that?"

What you're testing: do accountants already drive client tool choice? Your entire distribution model assumes they do. If clients show up with whatever they please and the accountant just copes, your channel doesn't exist.
Part 4 - The distribution question (the make-or-break assumption)
Now you can sketch the idea - briefly, neutrally, as a possibility, not a pitch:
"Suppose there were a tool where you could invite your clients, they'd capture receipts in a few seconds on their phone, and everything would land in one dashboard where you could see each client's status and export clean data at year-end. Set aside whether it exists or is any good for a second-"

"-would you actually invite your clients to something like that? Be honest." (Then SILENCE.)
"What would stop you?" (This is the gold question. Push for the real objection.)
"If you did, realistically how many of your clients would actually start using it - and how many would ignore it?" (This number is your unit economics. Write down the exact figure.)
"Who do you think should pay for something like that - you, or the client?"
"If the client paid, say, €5–10 a month, would they balk? Would you feel weird recommending something they have to pay for?"

What you're testing: the assumption that, if false, kills the whole idea - will they distribute it? Listen hard for the objections you're afraid of: "it threatens my billable hours," "I don't want to be tech support for clients," "I'm not telling clients to pay for something," "I trust Sage." Any of those, said sincerely, is a finding.
Part 5 - The tailwind (Hill 4, light touch)

"How are you thinking about the e-invoicing and real-time reporting rules coming in from 2028? Is that on your radar?"
"Is that something you're dreading, or already sorted?"

What you're testing: whether the regulatory wedge is something they'd pay to be ready for, or too far off to care about yet. Don't lead them; if they don't raise it as a worry, that's data too.
Part 6 - Close (and the magic final question)

"If I built this, would you want to be one of the first to try it with a couple of clients?" (A real yes = they offer a specific client or a date. A polite yes = vague enthusiasm. Note which.)
"Is there anyone else you'd suggest I talk to?" (Referrals are a signal of genuine interest - and your next interviews.)
"Anything I should have asked but didn't?"


How to read the results (don't skip this)
The trap is hearing what you want. Calibrate against these:
Strong signal (build it): they get animated describing the chase pain unprompted; they already move clients between tools; they name a specific painful niche client; they give a concrete onboarding number ("I'd put maybe 25 on day one"); they offer a client to pilot with or a referral. Enthusiasm tied to specifics and commitments is real.
Weak signal (don't be fooled): "Sounds great." "I'd definitely use that." "Cool idea." Generic future-tense praise with no specifics, no number, no name, no commitment. This is politeness, not validation - discount it entirely.
The kill signals (listen for these hardest): "Honestly clients are mostly on Xero/QuickBooks and it's fine." "I wouldn't want to be their tech support." "That's basically my billable work, why would I automate it away for free?" "I wouldn't ask clients to pay for something." Two or three accountants saying any of these sincerely means the accountant-first model has a hole - and you've found it for the price of a few coffees instead of a year of code.
A practical note: aim for 5 or so accountants, at least 2 with farm/equine clients. Take notes in their words (exact phrases, not your paraphrase - "shoebox of crap every January" is data; "expressed frustration with record-keeping" is you editorializing). And go in genuinely hoping to be proven wrong on the distribution question - that's the mindset that gets you the truth instead of the comfort.
One last honest flag: this script tests demand and distribution, not whether they'll pay and keep paying - that only a real pilot proves. But if you can't get past Part 4 with a few sincere yeses and a real onboarding number, there's no point building the pilot. Get through this first.
